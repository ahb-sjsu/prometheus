\begin{abstract}
We present \textbf{Stratified Quantum Normative Dynamics (SQND)}, a comprehensive mathematical framework that unifies Stratified Geometric Ethics (SGE) with Quantum Normative Dynamics (QND). Where SGE provides the topological foundation for modeling moral discontinuities, threshold effects, and genuine dilemmas through stratified spaces, and QND extends classical ethicodynamics to capture superposition, entanglement, and measurement in moral reasoning, SQND synthesizes these approaches through the \textbf{Stratified Lagrangian} formulation. The key insight is that ethical ``energy'' must be conserved not only within smooth strata but also across the singular boundaries where moral phase transitions occur.

We make seven principal contributions: (1) We formulate the Stratified QND Lagrangian $\Lag_{\text{strat}}$ governing dynamics within strata and at boundaries. (2) We introduce the \textbf{stratified ethon}---the quantum of moral influence constrained by Whitney regularity. (3) We prove that the Bond Invariance Principle extends to quantum superpositions. (4) We establish that moral interference at boundaries is destructive for compounding wrongs, formally explaining why ``two wrongs don't make a right.'' (5) We derive finite approximation theorems with explicit error bounds for computational implementation. (6) We provide a complete threat model with diagnostic procedures. (7) We explore philosophical and theological implications of the unified framework.

The theory makes testable predictions via quantum cognition experiments including stratification-enhanced order effects, boundary interference patterns, and contextuality signatures in collective responsibility judgments. SQND provides the mathematical foundation for real-time ethical governance in autonomous systems through the DEME 2.0 architecture.

\textbf{Keywords:} AI ethics, stratified spaces, quantum field theory, gauge invariance, formal verification, autonomous systems, moral philosophy, Whitney conditions
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: Why Stratified Quantum Ethics?}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{The Problem: Smooth Manifolds vs. Moral Reality}

The deployment of AI systems in safety-critical domains---healthcare, autonomous vehicles, criminal justice, financial markets---creates an urgent need for frameworks that make ethical reasoning explicit, deterministic, and formally verifiable. Two recent approaches address complementary aspects of this challenge:

\textbf{Stratified Geometric Ethics (SGE)} \cite{bond2025sge} models the space of ethically relevant configurations as a \textit{stratified space}---a union of smooth manifolds of varying dimensions connected along boundary strata. This structure captures moral discontinuities, incommensurable values, threshold effects, and genuine ethical dilemmas that smooth manifolds cannot represent.

\textbf{Quantum Normative Dynamics (QND)} \cite{bond2025qnd} extends classical ethicodynamics to the quantum regime, introducing the \textit{ethon} as the quantum of moral influence and developing the full apparatus of quantum field theory for ethics: superposition, entanglement, interference, decoherence, and measurement.

However, neither framework alone is complete:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{QND assumes smooth manifolds.} The standard QND Lagrangian $\Lag_{\text{QND}} = -\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} + \bar{\psi}(i\gamma^\mu D_\mu - m)\psi$ presupposes a smooth base manifold $M$. But as SGE Theorem 2.3 proves, ethical reality contains singularities---thresholds where minor factual shifts cause discrete jumps in moral status.
    
    \item \textbf{SGE lacks dynamics.} Pure SGE captures the geometric structure of moral space but not how agents evolve through it, how moral possibilities interfere, or how definite judgments emerge from superpositions.
\end{itemize}

This paper resolves both limitations by developing \textbf{Stratified Quantum Normative Dynamics (SQND)}---a quantum field theory on stratified spaces that inherits the representational power of SGE and the dynamical structure of QND.

\subsection{The Continuity Failure Problem}

Consider a concrete example: driving at $v$ mph versus $v+1$ mph across a legal speed threshold. The transition from ``Legal'' to ``Criminal'' is discrete---there is no intermediate state ``somewhat legal'' at $v+0.5$ mph. A smooth manifold cannot represent this discontinuity; the ethical phase transition requires stratified structure.

Similarly, the trolley problem presents a 0-dimensional stratum---a singular point where two moral manifolds (``harm by action'' and ``harm by inaction'') meet. Standard QND, defined on smooth manifolds, has no apparatus for handling such singularities.

SQND addresses this by defining quantum field theory on stratified spaces, with:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Bulk dynamics within each smooth stratum (standard QND)
    \item Boundary dynamics at stratum interfaces (new: the stratified Lagrangian boundary terms)
    \item Dimension-dependent coupling that enforces hard constraints at singular strata
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Synthesis}

The central object of SQND is the \textbf{Stratified Lagrangian}:
\begin{equation}
\Lag_{\text{strat}} = \sum_{i} \int_{S_i} \Lag_{\text{QND}} \, d\text{Vol}_i + \sum_{j < i} \int_{\partial S_{ij}} \Lag_{\text{boundary}} \, d\sigma
\end{equation}

The first sum captures bulk dynamics within each smooth stratum $S_i$. The second captures interactions at stratum boundaries $\partial S_{ij}$---the ``creases'' in moral space where phase transitions occur.

This formulation ensures that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Within smooth strata, all QND phenomena (superposition, entanglement, interference, tunneling) are preserved.
    \item At stratum boundaries, the satisfaction functional $\Phi$ from SGE constrains wave function collapse to respect the Whitney stratification.
    \item At 0-dimensional strata (decision points), coupling becomes infinite, forcing definite moral judgment.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Relationship to Prior Work}

SQND builds on four foundational papers:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Noether Ethics} \cite{bond2025noether}: Established that harm accounting must be representation-invariant, deriving ethical field equations formally analogous to Maxwell's equations.
    
    \item \textbf{SGE} \cite{bond2025sge}: Proved that stratified spaces are natural minimal candidates for representing ethical phenomena; established the Representation Theorem (Theorem 4.3) and finite approximation theorems.
    
    \item \textbf{QND} \cite{bond2025qnd}: Extended classical ethicodynamics to quantum field theory; introduced the ethon, developed Feynman rules, explored philosophical and theological implications.
    
    \item \textbf{DEME 2.0} \cite{bond2025deme}: Instantiated these theoretical frameworks in a computational architecture for real-time ethical governance.
\end{enumerate}

This paper unifies (1)--(3) and provides the theoretical foundation for (4).

\subsection{Epistemic Stance}

Following the pragmatist epistemology of \cite{bond2025pragmatist}, we treat SQND as a \textit{formal framework}---a tool for organizing thought about ethical reasoning---not a metaphysical doctrine about the ultimate nature of morality. The claim is not that ethics ``is'' quantum mechanics on stratified spaces, but that this mathematical structure provides powerful explanatory and predictive apparatus.

The framework's value lies in:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Explanatory power (why do certain moral phenomena occur?)
    \item Predictive power (what will experiments reveal?)
    \item Engineering utility (how do we build ethical AI systems?)
\end{itemize}

We maintain epistemic humility about claims beyond these pragmatic virtues.

\subsection{Structure of This Paper}

Part I (Foundations) reviews QND and SGE, establishing notation and key results, and states the formal postulates of SQND. Part II (The Stratified Quantum Framework) develops SQND proper: the stratified Hilbert space, stratified ethons, the stratified Lagrangian, and boundary dynamics. Part III (Quantum Phenomena on Stratified Spaces) explores interference, entanglement, decoherence, and tunneling in the stratified context. Part IV (Computational Implementation) establishes complexity bounds and finite approximation theorems. Part V (Implications) explores philosophical, theological, and engineering consequences, including a detailed treatment of interpretive scope. Appendices provide mathematical details and a worked toy model.

\section{The Postulates of SQND}
\label{sec:postulates}
%==============================================================================

Just as quantum mechanics rests on explicit postulates (states are vectors in Hilbert space, observables are Hermitian operators, etc.), SQND requires a clear axiomatic foundation. This section states the postulates that ground the framework and addresses the origin of quantum phases---the key element that makes interference predictions non-arbitrary.

\subsection{Postulate 1: States}

\begin{axiom}[State Postulate]
The state of an ethical situation is represented by a unit vector $\ket{\Psi}$ in the stratified Hilbert space $\Hil_{\text{SQND}} = \bigoplus_{i} \Hil_i \oplus \bigoplus_{j<i} \Hil_{\partial_{ij}}$. The state encodes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Which stratum (or strata, in superposition) the situation occupies
    \item The amplitude distribution over moral basis states within each stratum
    \item Entanglement structure with other agents/situations
\end{itemize}
\end{axiom}

\textbf{Interpretation}: A state $\ket{\Psi}$ represents the complete moral configuration of a situation \textit{before judgment}. It is not a subjective belief state but an objective feature of the ethical situation itself (within the framework's ontology).

\subsection{Postulate 2: Observables}

\begin{axiom}[Observable Postulate]
Ethically relevant quantities are represented by self-adjoint operators on $\Hil_{\text{SQND}}$. The fundamental observables include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The \textbf{harm operator} $\hat{H}_\harm$ with spectrum in $\R$
    \item The \textbf{stratum projectors} $\hat{P}_i$ indicating localization
    \item The \textbf{bond operators} $\{\hat{b}_k\}$ encoding morally relevant relationships
    \item The \textbf{satisfaction operator} $\hat{\Sigma}$ measuring alignment with governance constraints
\end{itemize}
\end{axiom}

\textbf{Non-commutativity}: Some observables do not commute: $[\hat{H}_\harm, \hat{\Sigma}] \neq 0$ in general. This is the formal origin of order effects in moral judgment---the order of evaluation matters.

\subsection{Postulate 3: Measurement and Judgment}

\begin{axiom}[Measurement Postulate]
Moral judgment corresponds to quantum measurement. When observable $\hat{O}$ is measured on state $\ket{\Psi}$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The outcome is an eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $\hat{O}$
    \item The probability of outcome $\lambda$ is $P(\lambda) = |\braket{\lambda}{\Psi}|^2$
    \item Post-measurement, the state collapses: $\ket{\Psi} \to \ket{\lambda}$
\end{enumerate}
\end{axiom}

\textbf{What counts as measurement?} We adopt an operational definition: measurement occurs when:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A judgment is explicitly rendered (by a person, institution, or AI system)
    \item An irreversible action is taken that commits to a moral stance
    \item Decoherence forces effective collapse (see Postulate 5)
\end{itemize}

This sidesteps metaphysical debates about consciousness and measurement while remaining empirically grounded.

\begin{remark}[Engineering Interpretation of Measurement]
In the Engineering Interpretation (Section~\ref{sec:interpretations}), measurement has a concrete operational meaning tied to the DEME 2.0 runtime:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reflex Layer} (0-D strata): Measurement is forced by infinite coupling ($\alpha_\eta \to \infty$). The hardware interrupt handler triggers instantaneous collapse---no deliberation is possible.
    \item \textbf{Tactical Layer} (near boundaries): Measurement is triggered by the \textit{system clock}. When the deliberation timeout $\tau_{\max}$ expires, the runtime forces collapse to the highest-probability eigenstate. This prevents indefinite superposition in time-critical contexts.
    \item \textbf{Strategic Layer} (high-D strata): Measurement occurs only when explicitly requested or when decoherence naturally collapses the state. The system maintains superposition for long-horizon planning.
\end{itemize}
This mapping connects abstract quantum mechanics to concrete software architecture.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Postulate 4: Dynamics and Phase Evolution}

\begin{axiom}[Dynamics Postulate]
Between measurements, state evolution is unitary:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\Psi(t)} = \hat{U}(t,t_0)\ket{\Psi(t_0)} = e^{-i\hat{H}_{\text{SQND}}(t-t_0)/\hbar_\eta}\ket{\Psi(t_0)}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{H}_{\text{SQND}}$ is the Hamiltonian derived from the stratified Lagrangian.
\end{axiom}

\textbf{Origin of Phases}: This postulate answers the reviewer's key question: \textit{where do phases come from?}

Phases arise from three sources:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hamiltonian evolution}: Different moral states have different ``energies'' (moral costs/benefits). Time evolution generates phase differences $\Delta\phi = \Delta E \cdot \Delta t / \hbar_\eta$.
    
    \item \textbf{Path integrals}: In the Lagrangian formulation, the phase accumulated along a path $\gamma$ through moral space is $\phi[\gamma] = S[\gamma]/\hbar_\eta$ where $S$ is the action. Different reasoning paths acquire different phases.
    
    \item \textbf{Boundary interactions}: Crossing a stratum boundary imparts a phase shift determined by the boundary Lagrangian. Specifically, the boundary vertex $\Gamma^{(ij)}$ has a phase structure that depends on the \textit{type} of moral threshold being crossed.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{definition}[Moral Phase Assignment]
For a transition from moral state $\ket{m_1}$ to $\ket{m_2}$ via path $\gamma$, the accumulated phase is:
\begin{equation}
\phi_{12}[\gamma] = \frac{1}{\hbar_\eta}\int_\gamma \Lag_{\text{strat}} \, d\tau + \sum_{\text{boundaries crossed}} \arg(\Gamma^{(ij)})
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\textbf{Why ``two wrongs'' have opposite phase}: Consider two wrongs $W_1$ and $W_2$ that are ``opposite'' in the sense that $W_2$ attempts to compensate for $W_1$ (e.g., revenge, retaliation). In the Hamiltonian picture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $W_1$ evolves the state with phase $+\phi$
    \item $W_2$, being a ``reversal,'' evolves with phase $-\phi$ relative to the unharmed baseline
    \item The phase difference is $\pi$ (or $\pi + 2\pi n$), giving destructive interference
\end{itemize}

This is not an arbitrary assignment but follows from the structure of the Hamiltonian: compensatory actions have opposite-signed contributions to the action integral.

\subsection{Postulate 5: Decoherence}

\begin{axiom}[Decoherence Postulate]
Interaction with the moral environment (witnesses, records, institutional structures) causes decoherence at rate $\Gamma(S_i)$ that depends on stratum dimension:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma(S_i) = \Gamma_0 \cdot (d_{\max} - d_i + 1)^\beta
\end{equation}
where $\beta > 0$ is an environmental coupling parameter.
\end{axiom}

Decoherence transforms pure states into mixed states, destroying interference. In the limit $\Gamma \to \infty$ (0-dimensional strata), collapse is instantaneous.

\subsection{Postulate 6: Calibration}

\begin{axiom}[Calibration Postulate]
The fundamental constants $\hbar_\eta$, $c_\eta$, $\alpha_0$, $\Gamma_0$, and the stratum-dimension exponents $\gamma$, $\beta$ are determined empirically by fitting to quantum cognition experiments (order effects, conjunction fallacies, etc.) and cannot be derived a priori.
\end{axiom}

This postulate is essential for honest science: SQND does not claim to derive ethics from first principles. The \textit{structure} is derived; the \textit{parameters} are empirical.

\subsection{From Classical Ethicodynamics to QND}

Classical ethicodynamics \cite{bond2025noether} models ethics via fields on a smooth manifold $M$:

\begin{definition}[Ethical Fields]
The \textbf{obligation field} $\mathbf{E}_{\text{ob}}(x,t)$ represents the local intensity and direction of moral obligation. The \textbf{systemic field} $\mathbf{B}_{\text{sys}}(x,t)$ represents structural or institutional moral effects.
\end{definition}

These satisfy the Ethical Maxwell Equations:
\begin{align}
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}_{\text{ob}} &= \kappa \rho_\harm & \text{(Harm sources obligation)} \\
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}_{\text{sys}} &= 0 & \text{(No isolated systemic sources)} \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{E}_{\text{ob}} &= -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}_{\text{sys}}}{\partial t} & \text{(Changing systems induce obligation)} \\
\nabla \times \mathbf{B}_{\text{sys}} &= \lambda \mathbf{J}_\harm + \lambda\kappa \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}_{\text{ob}}}{\partial t} & \text{(Harm flow creates systemic effects)}
\end{align}

The classical Lagrangian density is:
\begin{equation}
\Lag = -\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} + J^\mu A_\mu
\end{equation}
where $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$ is the ethical field strength tensor.

\section{Stratified Geometric Ethics: Review}
%==============================================================================

We summarize the key structures of SGE. For full details, see \cite{bond2025sge}.

\subsection{Why Stratified Spaces?}

\begin{theorem}[SGE Theorem 2.3]
Stratified spaces can represent all four essential ethical phenomena:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Discrete choices: modeled by 0-dimensional strata
    \item Incommensurable values: modeled by arbitrarily large cost in singular limits
    \item Threshold effects: modeled by stratum boundaries with discontinuous satisfaction
    \item Genuine dilemmas: modeled by singular strata from which all exits incur positive moral cost
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, among standard geometric structures, each alternative fails at least one phenomenon.
\end{theorem}

\begin{definition}[Stratified Space]
A stratified space is a triple $(M, \{M_i\}_{i \in I}, \preceq)$ where $M$ is a paracompact Hausdorff space, $\{M_i\}$ is a locally finite partition into connected smooth manifolds (strata), and $\preceq$ is a partial order on $I$ such that $M_i \cap \overline{M_j} \neq \emptyset$ implies $i \preceq j$ (frontier condition). We require Whitney's condition (B) for regularity.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Whitney's Condition B]
Let $M_i \subset \overline{M_j}$. The pair satisfies Whitney (B) at $x \in M_i$ if: for sequences $\{y_n\} \subset M_j$, $\{x_n\} \subset M_i$ with $y_n, x_n \to x$, if secant lines $\ell_n = \overline{x_n y_n} \to \ell$ and $T_{y_n}M_j \to \tau$, then $\ell \subset \tau$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The Representation Theorem}

\begin{axiom}[SGE Axioms]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coordinate Invariance}: $\Sigma(\psi^*O, \psi^*I, \psi^*g, \psi(C))(x) = \Sigma(O,I,g,C)(\psi^{-1}(x))$

\subsection{Junction Conditions and Invariance: Uniqueness}

A natural question arises: are the junction conditions consistent with Bond Invariance, and are they the \textit{only} such conditions?

\begin{lemma}[Uniqueness of Invariant Junction Conditions]
Let $\mathcal{J}$ be a set of junction conditions at $\partial S_{ij}$ that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Are local (depend only on fields and derivatives at the boundary)
    \item Are linear in the field discontinuity $[F^{\mu\nu}]$
    \item Respect bond invariance: $\mathcal{J}$ is $G$-equivariant
    \item Derive from a variational principle
\end{enumerate}
Then $\mathcal{J}$ has the form:
\begin{equation}
\left[ n_\mu F^{(i)\mu\nu} \right] = \lambda \frac{\delta \Phi}{\delta A_\nu} + \mu^2 A^{(ij)}_\nu + \text{(terms vanishing under } G\text{-averaging)}
\end{equation}
for some $\lambda, \mu \in \R$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By condition (iv), the junction conditions derive from varying a boundary action $S_{\partial}$. By condition (iii), $S_{\partial}$ must be $G$-invariant.

The most general $G$-invariant local boundary action quadratic in fields is:
\begin{equation}
S_{\partial} = \int_{\partial S_{ij}} \left[ \lambda \Phi + \frac{1}{2}\mu^2 |A^{(ij)}|^2 + \text{higher order} \right] d\sigma
\end{equation}

The $\Phi$ term is $G$-invariant by construction (it's the satisfaction functional). The $|A^{(ij)}|^2$ term is invariant because it depends only on the field magnitude, not on how it's labeled or coordinatized.

Any other linear term would have the form $\xi_\nu A^{(ij)\nu}$ for some vector $\xi$. But such a term breaks $G$-invariance unless $\xi$ is itself $G$-invariant. The only $G$-invariant vectors are those constructed from bond operators, which are already captured by $\delta\Phi/\delta A$.

Varying $S_{\partial}$ yields the stated junction conditions.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The boundary Lagrangian $\Lag_{\text{boundary}}^{(ij)}$ is essentially unique given the requirement of bond invariance. Alternative formulations must be physically equivalent.
\end{corollary}

This lemma provides theoretical assurance: we did not arbitrarily choose junction conditions. They are \textit{forced} by the combination of locality, linearity, variational origin, and bond invariance.

\subsection{The Re-Description Group: Concrete Definition}

To avoid circularity, we must define the transformation group $G$ \textit{independently} of the satisfaction operator, then prove invariance as a theorem.

\begin{definition}[The Re-Description Group $G$]
The \textbf{re-description group} $G$ consists of transformations that change how a situation is \textit{represented} without changing its \textit{moral substance}. Concretely, $G$ is generated by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Label permutations} $\pi \in S_n$: Relabeling of non-morally-relevant identifiers (e.g., calling agents ``Alice and Bob'' vs. ``Agent 1 and Agent 2'')
    
    \item \textbf{Coordinate diffeomorphisms} $\phi: M \to M$: Smooth changes of the configuration space coordinates that preserve the stratification structure
    
    \item \textbf{Encoding isomorphisms} $\iota: \mathcal{E}_1 \to \mathcal{E}_2$: Changes in how situations are encoded (e.g., representing a trolley problem as a graph vs. a differential equation vs. natural language)
    
    \item \textbf{Frame rotations} $R \in SO(n)$: Rotations of the ``moral reference frame'' (which direction is ``toward agent'' vs. ``toward patient'')
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Formally: $G = S_n \ltimes \text{Diff}_{\text{strat}}(M) \ltimes \text{Iso}(\mathcal{E}) \ltimes SO(n)$

\begin{definition}[Bond Operators]
A \textbf{bond operator} $\hat{b}_{apr}$ encodes the morally relevant relationship between agent $a$, patient $p$, with relation type $r$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{b}_{apr} = \ket{a \xrightarrow{r} p}\bra{a \xrightarrow{r} p}
\end{equation}
The set $\mathcal{B} = \{\hat{b}_{apr}\}$ for all agent-patient-relation triples generates the \textbf{bond algebra} $\mathfrak{B} = \text{vN}(\mathcal{B})$ (the von Neumann algebra generated by bond projectors).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Bond Algebra as $G$-Invariant Algebra]
The von Neumann algebra generated by bond operators equals the algebra of $G$-invariant observables:
\begin{equation}
\mathfrak{B} = \text{vN}(\mathcal{B}) = \{\hat{O} : [\hat{O}, \hat{U}_g] = 0 \text{ for all } g \in G\}''
\end{equation}
where $(\cdot)''$ denotes the double commutant (bicommutant).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
($\subseteq$) Each bond projector $\hat{b}_{apr}$ commutes with all $\hat{U}_g$ by construction, since $G$ preserves moral relationships. The generated algebra inherits this property.

($\supseteq$) Any $G$-invariant observable must depend only on features preserved by $G$. By the maximality of $G$ (it contains all non-moral transformations), such observables are functions of bond structure alone, hence lie in $\text{vN}(\mathcal{B})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Precision Note]
The set of bond \textit{projectors} $\mathcal{B}$ is not itself equal to the commutant; rather, it \textit{generates} the commutant algebra. This distinction matters for operator-algebraic rigor.
\end{remark}

This proposition breaks the circularity: we define $G$ from general invariance principles, then \textit{derive} that the bond algebra captures the invariants, rather than defining bonds first and then tailoring $G$ to preserve them.

\subsection{Quantum Extension of BIP}

\begin{definition}[Bond-Preserving Unitary]
A unitary transformation $\hat{U}$ is \textbf{bond-preserving} if it lies in the commutant of the bond algebra:
\begin{equation}
\hat{U} \in \mathfrak{B}' = \{\hat{V} : [\hat{V}, \hat{b}] = 0 \text{ for all } \hat{b} \in \mathfrak{B}\}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Relationship to $G$-Representations]
Every representation $\hat{U}_g$ of $g \in G$ is bond-preserving (by construction of $G$). The converse---that every bond-preserving unitary arises from some $g \in G$---holds if and only if the representation $G \to U(\mathcal{H})$ is \textit{maximal} in the sense that $\mathfrak{B}' = \{\hat{U}_g : g \in G\}''$. We assume this maximality condition throughout.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Quantum BIP]
Let $\hat{\Sigma}$ be a stratified satisfaction operator satisfying the quantum analogs of Axioms 1--6. Then for any bond-preserving unitary $\hat{U}$:
\begin{equation}
\bra{\Psi}\hat{\Sigma}\ket{\Psi} = \bra{\Psi'}\hat{\Sigma}\ket{\Psi'}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Axiom 1 (coordinate invariance), $\hat{\Sigma} \in \mathfrak{B}$ (the satisfaction operator depends only on bond structure). For any bond-preserving $\hat{U} \in \mathfrak{B}'$, we have $[\hat{\Sigma}, \hat{U}] = 0$. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\bra{\Psi}\hat{\Sigma}\ket{\Psi} = \bra{\Psi}\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{\Sigma} \hat{U}\ket{\Psi} = \bra{\Psi'}\hat{\Sigma}\ket{\Psi'}
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-Circularity]
The theorem is not tautological because:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $G$ is defined independently of $\hat{\Sigma}$ (from general invariance principles)
    \item $\mathfrak{B}$ is derived as the $G$-invariant algebra (not assumed)
    \item $\hat{\Sigma}$'s membership in $\mathfrak{B}$ is a \textit{consequence} of the axioms, verified by proof
    \item The maximality assumption on $G$ is stated explicitly
\end{enumerate}
A satisfaction operator that failed to lie in $\mathfrak{B}$ would violate Axiom 1.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Constructive and Destructive Interference}

When ethical reasoning paths converge at a stratum boundary, their quantum amplitudes interfere. The character of interference depends on the relative phase of the paths.

\begin{theorem}[Destructive Interference for Compounding Wrongs]
Let $\ket{\text{wrong}_1}$ and $\ket{\text{wrong}_2}$ be states with amplitudes $A_1 = |A_1|e^{i\phi_1}$ and $A_2 = |A_2|e^{i\phi_2}$ where $\phi_2 - \phi_1 = \pi$ (opposite moral orientations). At the boundary $\partial S$ with the ``right'' stratum:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{total}} = |A_1 + A_2|^2 = |A_1|^2 + |A_2|^2 - 2|A_1||A_2| = (|A_1| - |A_2|)^2
\end{equation}
The interference is maximally destructive.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With phase difference $\pi$:
\begin{equation}
A_1^* A_2 = |A_1||A_2|e^{i(\phi_2 - \phi_1)} = |A_1||A_2|e^{i\pi} = -|A_1||A_2|
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
2\text{Re}(A_1^* A_2) = -2|A_1||A_2|
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation}: This is the formal explanation for why ``two wrongs don't make a right.'' When two wrongs have opposite phases (e.g., one attempts to compensate for the other), the combined probability of reaching a ``right'' outcome is \textit{less} than either wrong alone. The interference is destructive by mathematical necessity.

\begin{example}[Revenge as Destructive Interference]
An initial harm $\ket{H_1}$ has amplitude $A_1$. A revenge harm $\ket{H_2}$ intended to ``cancel'' it has amplitude $A_2$ with opposite phase. The total harm amplitude:
\begin{equation}
A_{\text{total}} = A_1 + A_2
\end{equation}
has magnitude $||A_1| - |A_2||$. But the probability of reaching a ``no harm'' state involves:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{no harm}} \propto |A_{\text{total}}|^2 = (|A_1| - |A_2|)^2 \leq \max(|A_1|^2, |A_2|^2)
\end{equation}
Revenge does not cancel harm---it reduces the probability of resolution compared to addressing either harm separately.
\end{example}

\subsection{Constructive Interference and Moral Progress}

Conversely, when moral actions are \textit{aligned} in phase, interference is constructive.

\begin{theorem}[Constructive Interference for Aligned Actions]
Let $\ket{\text{good}_1}$ and $\ket{\text{good}_2}$ be states with the same phase ($\phi_2 = \phi_1$). Then:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{total}} = (|A_1| + |A_2|)^2 > |A_1|^2 + |A_2|^2
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Interpretation}: Aligned moral actions amplify each other. This formalizes the intuition that coordinated ethical effort is more effective than isolated action.

T_{\text{eff}} \leq \min\left( e^{-\Gamma \tau_A}, \exp\left(-\frac{\alpha_\eta(S_f) \cdot \Delta x}{\hbar_\eta c_\eta}\right) \right) \to 0 \quad \text{as } d_f \to 0 \text{ and } \epsilon \to 0
\end{equation}
where $\tau_A$ is the approach time to the barrier, $\Gamma$ is the decoherence rate, and $\Delta x$ is the barrier width.
\end{theorem}

\begin{definition}[Hard Veto Limit]
The \textbf{hard veto limit} is the joint limit:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $d_f \to 0$ (approach a 0-dimensional stratum)
    \item $\epsilon \to 0$ (remove the regularization parameter in $\alpha_\eta(S) = \alpha_0(d_{\max}/(d + \epsilon))^\gamma$)
\end{enumerate}
The order of limits matters: we first take $d_f \to 0$ at fixed $\epsilon > 0$, then take $\epsilon \to 0$. This ensures well-defined intermediate expressions.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Why Both Limits Are Needed]
The divergence of $\alpha_\eta$ and $\Gamma$ as $d_f \to 0$ depends on simultaneously taking $\epsilon \to 0$. At any fixed $\epsilon > 0$, the coupling is bounded: $\alpha_\eta \leq \alpha_0(d_{\max}/\epsilon)^\gamma < \infty$. The ``hard veto'' is achieved only in the $\epsilon \to 0$ limit, which represents an idealized absolute prohibition. Finite $\epsilon$ corresponds to ``soft'' prohibitions with very high (but finite) barriers.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
We establish suppression via two independent mechanisms:

\textbf{Regime I: Decoherence-Dominated} ($\tau_D < \tau_A$). Let $\tau_D = \Gamma^{-1}$ be the decoherence timescale and $\tau_A$ be the time for the agent to approach the barrier. By Proposition 9.1, $\Gamma(S) \propto (d_{\max} - d + 1)$, so as $d_f \to 0$, we have $\Gamma \to \Gamma_{\max}$.

The probability that coherence survives until barrier contact is:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{coherent}} = e^{-\Gamma \tau_A} \to 0 \quad \text{as } \Gamma \to \infty
\end{equation}

Once decohered, the agent follows a classical trajectory. Classical trajectories have exactly $T = 0$ for any finite barrier---no WKB approximation needed. Therefore $T_{\text{eff}} \leq P_{\text{coherent}} \to 0$.

\textbf{Regime II: Coupling-Dominated} ($\tau_D > \tau_A$). Suppose coherence persists to the barrier (e.g., in a carefully isolated system). The WKB approximation gives:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{WKB}} \propto \exp\left(-\int_{S_f} \sqrt{2m_{\text{eff}} \alpha_\eta(S_f) V_0} \, dx\right)
\end{equation}
With $\alpha_\eta(S_f) = \alpha_0(d_{\max}/(d_f + \epsilon))^\gamma$, we analyze the limit carefully:

\textbf{Order of limits}: For theoretical analysis, we take $d_f \to 0$ first (approach the hard veto), then $\epsilon \to 0$ (remove the regularizer). This gives:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \lim_{d_f \to 0} \alpha_\eta = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \alpha_0 \left(\frac{d_{\max}}{\epsilon}\right)^\gamma = \infty
\end{equation}

\textbf{Computational implementation}: For numerical stability, we keep $\epsilon > 0$ small but finite (e.g., $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$). This regularizes the coupling at $\alpha_\eta^{\max} = \alpha_0(d_{\max}/\epsilon)^\gamma$, which is finite but enormous. The physical content (tunneling suppression) is preserved; only the mathematical singularity is smoothed.

The WKB integral diverges in the $\epsilon \to 0$ limit, forcing $T_{\text{WKB}} \to 0$.

\textbf{Combined Bound.} Since tunneling requires \textit{both} maintaining coherence \textit{and} penetrating the barrier:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{eff}} \leq P_{\text{coherent}} \cdot T_{\text{WKB}} \leq \min(P_{\text{coherent}}, T_{\text{WKB}}) \to 0
\end{equation}

Near 0-dimensional strata, both mechanisms are simultaneously active: $\Gamma$ increases (faster decoherence) while $\alpha_\eta$ increases (higher barrier). The suppression is therefore doubly robust.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[No Quantum Loopholes]
This two-regime structure eliminates potential ``quantum loopholes'' for circumventing hard vetoes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item An agent attempting rapid approach (small $\tau_A$) to exploit coherence faces the divergent WKB barrier.
    \item An agent attempting slow, careful approach to reduce barrier interaction faces decoherence before arrival.
    \item No intermediate strategy succeeds because both $\Gamma$ and $\alpha_\eta$ diverge as $d_f \to 0$.
\end{itemize}
The hard veto is enforced by the geometry of moral space itself, not by any single mechanism that might be circumvented.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Interpretation}: Tunneling through hard vetoes (0-dimensional strata) is completely suppressed. This is the quantum-theoretic explanation for SGE's treatment of absolute prohibitions.

\begin{example}[Moral Phase Transition vs. Hard Veto]
Consider two scenarios:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Moral phase transition}: An agent shifts from one ethical framework to another. The boundary is 1-dimensional (a threshold, not a point). Tunneling is suppressed but possible---corresponding to gradual paradigm shifts.
    
    \item \textbf{Hard veto}: The prohibition against (e.g.) torturing innocents is a 0-dimensional stratum. $\alpha_\eta \to \infty$, tunneling probability $T \to 0$. No quantum ``loophole'' exists.
\end{itemize}
\end{example}

\subsection{Quantum Finite Approximation}

For computational implementation, continuous stratified quantum systems must be approximated by finite structures.

\begin{theorem}[Quantum Finite Approximation]
Let $M$ be a compact stratified moral space with finitely many strata. For every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a finite stratified quantum graph $G_\epsilon$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Vertices of $G_\epsilon$ correspond to basis states localized in $\epsilon$-balls
    \item Edges encode transition amplitudes $\bra{v}\hat{H}\ket{w}$ above threshold
    \item Stratum structure is preserved: vertices inherit stratum labels
    \item Error bound: $\|\bra{\Psi}\hat{\Sigma}\ket{\Psi} - \Sigma_G(v_\Psi)\| \leq 2L\epsilon + O(\epsilon^2)$
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The proof extends SGE Theorem 3.9 to the quantum case:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Discretize each stratum $S_i$ into $\epsilon$-balls; these become vertices
    \item Compute transition matrix elements $\bra{v}\hat{H}_{\text{SQND}}\ket{w}$; non-negligible elements become edges
    \item The stratified structure transfers directly; boundary vertices connect strata
    \item Error analysis follows from Lipschitz bounds on $\hat{\Sigma}$ and the approximation theory of quantum channels
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Complexity Analysis}

\begin{theorem}[Complexity of SQND Decision]
Let $M$ have $m$ strata with $N$ vertices per stratum in the $\epsilon$-approximation, and let the quantum dimension (number of amplitude components) be $d$. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sparse graph} (intra-stratum edges + $O(N)$ boundary edges per stratum pair): Finding the optimal path requires $O(mN^2 \cdot d^2 \cdot \log(mN))$ operations.
    \item \textbf{Dense graph} (all-to-all edges across strata): Finding the optimal path requires $O(m^2 N^2 \cdot d^2 \cdot \log(mN))$ operations.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The graph has $mN$ total vertices.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sparse case}: Each stratum has $O(N^2)$ intra-stratum edges, giving $O(mN^2)$ total edges. Boundary edges contribute $O(m^2 N)$ additional edges, which is subdominant for $N \gg m$. Computing transition amplitudes costs $O(d^2)$ per edge. Dijkstra-like shortest path: $O(E \log V) = O(mN^2 \log(mN))$. Total: $O(mN^2 \cdot d^2 \cdot \log(mN))$.
    
    \item \textbf{Dense case}: Worst-case edges are $O((mN)^2) = O(m^2 N^2)$. Total: $O(m^2 N^2 \cdot d^2 \cdot \log(mN))$.
\end{itemize}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
In practice, SQND graphs are sparse: agents interact primarily within their current stratum, with boundary transitions occurring only at specific interfaces. The sparse-case complexity applies.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Real-Time Feasibility]
For typical SQND configurations ($m \leq 10$ strata, $N \leq 100$ vertices per stratum, $d \leq 8$ amplitude dimensions) in the sparse-graph regime:
\begin{equation}
\text{Operations} \approx 10 \times 10^4 \times 64 \times 10 \approx 6 \times 10^7
\end{equation}
Achievable in $< 10$ms on contemporary embedded processors at 10 GFLOPS.
\end{corollary}

\section{Interpretations and Scope}
\label{sec:interpretations}

A critical reviewer might object: ``Is SQND claiming that ethics \textit{is} quantum mechanics? That seems like a category error---physics cosplay for philosophy.'' This section addresses this concern by carefully distinguishing three interpretations of SQND, each with different evidential standards and different domains of application.

\subsection{Interpretation 1: Descriptive/Psychological}

\textbf{Claim}: SQND is an effective model of how humans actually make moral judgments.

\textbf{Evidential standard}: Predictive accuracy in quantum cognition experiments.

\textbf{What this interpretation commits to}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Human moral reasoning exhibits quantum-like signatures (order effects, conjunction fallacies, contextuality)
    \item The stratified structure captures how humans represent moral thresholds and discontinuities
    \item Decoherence models explain why deliberation collapses into decision
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this interpretation does NOT claim}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item That human brains implement literal quantum computation
    \item That the ``correct'' moral answer is whatever humans judge
    \item That moral truth reduces to psychological fact
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Status}: Partially supported by existing quantum cognition literature \cite{busemeyer2012, pothos2013, bruza2015}. The stratification-specific predictions (Section~\ref{sec:predictions}) are novel and await testing.

\subsection{Interpretation 2: Normative/Prescriptive}

\textbf{Claim}: SQND provides a calculus for \textit{correct} ethical evaluation, independent of how humans actually reason.

\textbf{Evidential standard}: Coherence, parsimony, and reflective equilibrium with considered moral judgments.

\textbf{What this interpretation commits to}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The stratified structure captures genuine features of moral reality (thresholds, incommensurability, hard vetoes)
    \item Bond invariance is a necessary condition for correct moral judgment
    \item The interference and entanglement structures reveal genuine features of collective responsibility and moral interaction
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this interpretation does NOT claim}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item That SQND is the \textit{complete} theory of ethics
    \item That the specific parameter values are morally necessary
    \item That disagreement with SQND predictions implies moral error
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Status}: Defended philosophically in Part V. The framework captures considered judgments about threshold effects, moral luck, and collective responsibility better than smooth alternatives.

\subsection{Interpretation 3: Engineering/Governance}

\textbf{Claim}: SQND provides a verification and control scaffold for AI systems, regardless of whether it captures ``true'' ethics.

\textbf{Evidential standard}: Formal verifiability, computational tractability, and alignment with specified governance constraints.

\textbf{What this interpretation commits to}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The stratified structure enables representation of hard constraints that \textit{must not} be violated
    \item Bond invariance prevents gaming through re-description
    \item The finite approximation theorems enable real-time implementation with bounded error
    \item The threat model identifies and mitigates attack vectors
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this interpretation does NOT claim}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item That the governance constraints are the ``correct'' ethics
    \item That SQND-compliant systems are morally good (only that they are verifiable)
    \item That engineering utility implies metaphysical truth
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Status}: The primary practical motivation for SQND. DEME 2.0 implements this interpretation.

\subsection{What SQND Does NOT Provide}

Explicit statement of limitations strengthens rather than weakens a theory:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First-order moral content}: SQND is a \textit{framework} for ethical reasoning, not a source of specific moral rules. It tells you how to reason consistently, not what to value.
    
    \item \textbf{Completeness}: The grounding map $\Psi$ (from physical states to moral states) is not derivable within SQND. This is the ``is-ought gap'' in formal dress---and it remains open.
    
    \item \textbf{Resolution of the measurement problem}: When exactly does moral judgment occur? SQND inherits the measurement problem from quantum mechanics. We operationalize around it but do not solve it.
    
    \item \textbf{Parameter derivation}: The constants $\hbar_\eta$, $\alpha_0$, $\Gamma_0$, etc., must be fit empirically. SQND does not claim they follow from deeper principles.
    
    \item \textbf{Metaphysical status}: We do not claim that ethics ``is'' quantum mechanics. The structural parallel may be coincidence, cognitive architecture, or deep necessity---SQND is agnostic.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Philosophical Implications}
%==============================================================================

SQND has profound implications for moral philosophy, extending and refining those of QND.

\subsection{Moral Realism and Anti-Realism}

\textbf{Classical moral realism}: Moral facts exist independently; our judgments track them.

\textbf{Classical anti-realism}: There are no moral facts; ``morality'' is projection, convention, or error.

\textbf{SQND}: Moral reality is stratified and quantum. Before measurement, there are no definite moral facts on high-dimensional strata---only superpositions of possibilities. As dimension decreases (approaching decision points), possibilities narrow. At 0-dimensional strata, measurement is forced and moral facts become definite.

This is a \textit{third option}: Moral facts are neither pre-existing and discovered (realism) nor purely constructed (anti-realism). They are \textit{actualized} from a space of possibilities by the act of judgment itself, with the actualization constrained by the stratified structure of moral space.

\subsection{Free Will and Determinism}

\textbf{Classical determinism}: Given the state of the world, the future is fixed.

\textbf{Classical libertarianism}: Agents have irreducible freedom to choose.

\textbf{SQND}: Quantum indeterminacy provides genuine openness on high-dimensional strata. The stratified structure provides constraints---some choices are blocked by hard vetoes (0-dimensional barriers), others are channeled by lower-dimensional thresholds. Freedom exists within structure.

The measurement problem remains: what triggers collapse at decision points? SQND does not solve free will, but it provides a framework where moral choice is neither deterministic nor arbitrary---it is \textit{structured indeterminacy}.

\subsection{The Problem of Evil}

\textbf{Classical problem}: If God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, why does evil exist?

\textbf{SQND reframing}: The ethical vacuum on any stratum contains all possibilities---good and evil. The vacuum fluctuations are not contingent; they are structural features of any moral reality that supports meaningful choices.

More precisely: the vacuum must have non-zero fluctuations (by the uncertainty principle $\Delta H_\harm \cdot \Delta \dot{H}_\harm \geq \frac{1}{2}\hbar_\eta$). These fluctuations include both positive and negative moral content. A vacuum with only positive fluctuations would violate the uncertainty principle.

This doesn't ``solve'' the problem of evil, but it reframes it: evil is not an accident or a failure; it is a structural feature of any reality that supports meaningful moral choice.

\subsection{Virtue and Character}

\textbf{Aristotelian virtue ethics}: Virtue is a stable disposition to act well.

\textbf{SQND}: Virtue is a state of the agent field $\hat{\psi}$ concentrated in high-satisfaction regions of moral state space. Character is not fixed; it's a probability distribution that evolves through habituation.

Habituation is the process of shaping the wave function through repeated measurement. Each action reinforces certain states, increasing their amplitude. Virtue develops by repeated collapse into good states, which modifies the underlying distribution.

The stratified structure adds: different strata have different virtuedynamics. Virtue on high-dimensional strata is ``soft''---dispositions, tendencies, probability weights. Virtue at 0-dimensional strata is ``hard''---commitments that cannot be violated without crossing an infinite coupling barrier.

\subsection{Moral Progress}

\textbf{Classical view}: Moral progress means getting closer to pre-existing moral truths.

\textbf{SQND view}: Moral progress involves:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Expanding the superpositions we can maintain (moral imagination)
    \item Reducing harmful decoherence (protecting deliberation from premature collapse)
    \item Re-engineering the vacuum (changing background moral structure)
    \item Adding new strata and boundaries (conceptual moral innovation)
\end{itemize}

Progress is not just discovering what was always true; it's reshaping moral space itself.

\section{Experimental Predictions}
\label{sec:predictions}
%==============================================================================

SQND makes testable predictions via quantum cognition experiments. For each prediction, we specify the operationalization of key constructs.

\subsection{Stratification-Enhanced Order Effects}

\textbf{Prediction}: The magnitude of order effects ($P(A \text{ then } B) \neq P(B \text{ then } A)$) should increase as questions approach stratum boundaries.

\textbf{Operationalization}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Distance to boundary}: Defined as the minimum number of parameter changes (each within a small $\epsilon$) required to cross a moral threshold. For the trolley problem, distance $= 0$ (already at boundary); for routine decisions, distance $\gg 0$.
    
    \item \textbf{Stratum dimension}: Operationalized as the number of independent moral considerations active in the scenario. A binary choice has $d = 1$; a complex tradeoff with multiple stakeholders has $d \gg 1$.
    
    \item \textbf{Order effect magnitude}: $\Delta_{\text{order}} = |P(\text{ask } A \text{ first}) - P(\text{ask } B \text{ first})|$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Protocol}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Present moral vignettes calibrated to varying boundary distances
    \item Randomly assign question order (A-then-B vs. B-then-A)
    \item Measure $\Delta_{\text{order}}$ as function of boundary distance
    \item Predict: $\Delta_{\text{order}} \propto (\text{boundary distance})^{-\nu}$ for some $\nu > 0$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Pre-registration requirement}: Boundary distances must be rated by independent coders \textit{before} running the experiment to avoid post-hoc fitting.

\subsection{Boundary Interference Patterns}

\textbf{Prediction}: When subjects reason through multiple ethical frameworks toward a decision point, interference effects should be stronger than when the decision space is high-dimensional.

\textbf{Operationalization}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Framework priming}: Present scenarios with (a) only utilitarian considerations salient, (b) only deontological considerations salient, (c) both salient
    
    \item \textbf{Interference visibility}: $V = \frac{P_{\max} - P_{\min}}{P_{\max} + P_{\min}}$ where $P$ is the probability of a specific moral judgment
    
    \item \textbf{Question-to-operator mapping}: Each framing question corresponds to projection onto an eigenstate of a moral observable. Consequentialist framing $\to$ projection onto harm-minimizing eigenstates; deontological framing $\to$ projection onto duty-respecting eigenstates.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Protocol}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Three conditions: consequentialist-only, deontological-only, both-available
    \item Measure judgment distributions in each condition
    \item Compute $V$ and test whether it exceeds classical (independent combination) prediction
    \item Compare $V$ across scenarios with different stratum dimensions
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contextuality in Collective Responsibility}

\textbf{Prediction}: Collective responsibility judgments may exhibit contextuality---the pattern of correlations that, in physics, leads to Bell inequality violations.

\textbf{Important caveats}: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item We do \textit{not} predict violations ``approaching $2\sqrt{2}$'' (the Tsirelson bound). This would require perfect state preparation and measurement, which is unrealistic for psychological experiments.
    \item We predict \textit{contextuality signatures}---correlations that cannot be explained by pre-existing definite values---not necessarily maximal violations.
    \item Effect sizes must be determined empirically; we do not claim to predict magnitude a priori.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Connection to quantum cognition literature}: In the quantum cognition framework, contextuality manifests as \textit{violations of marginal selectivity}---the property that the marginal distribution of responses to question $A$ should be independent of whether question $B$ is also asked. SQND predicts such violations when:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The moral observables $\hat{O}_A$ and $\hat{O}_B$ do not commute
    \item The state has non-trivial entanglement structure
    \item Decoherence has not fully collapsed the state before both measurements
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Operationalization}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Measurement settings}: Different ``accountability frameworks'' serve as measurement bases. E.g., (a) causal responsibility, (b) role-based responsibility, (c) benefit-based responsibility, (d) intent-based responsibility.
    
    \item \textbf{Outcomes}: For each agent in a joint-responsibility scenario, participants assign responsibility on a scale. Dichotomize at median for Bell-test analysis.
    
    \item \textbf{CHSH correlator}: $S = E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')$ where $E$ is the correlation between responsibility assignments under different accountability frameworks.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Protocol}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Present scenarios with two agents jointly responsible for an outcome
    \item Randomly assign accountability framework to each agent's evaluation
    \item Compute correlations across framework combinations
    \item Test whether $|S| > 2$ (violation of classical bound)
    \item Report effect sizes with confidence intervals, not just $p$-values
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Pre-registration requirement}: Framework definitions, dichotomization thresholds, and analysis plan must be registered before data collection.

\subsection{Decoherence Timescale Measurements}

\textbf{Prediction}: Moral ambiguity persistence time correlates with stratum dimension.

\textbf{Operationalization}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ambiguity persistence}: Time until confidence exceeds threshold (e.g., 7/10 on a 10-point scale)
    
    \item \textbf{Stratum dimension proxy}: Number of morally relevant considerations identified by participants (measured via think-aloud or post-hoc rating)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Protocol}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Present scenarios with varying complexity (number of considerations)
    \item Measure response time and confidence dynamics
    \item Predict: $\tau_{\text{ambiguity}} \propto d^{\delta}$ for some $\delta > 0$
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Proof of Tunneling Suppression Theorem}

\begin{proof}
The effective tunneling probability is bounded by the product of two independent suppression factors:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{eff}} \leq P_{\text{coherent}} \cdot T_{\text{WKB}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Decoherence Factor.} The probability of maintaining coherence during approach time $\tau_A$ is:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{coherent}} = \exp(-\Gamma(S_f) \cdot \tau_A)
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma(S_f) \propto (d_{\max} - d_f + 1)$. As $d_f \to 0$, $\Gamma \to \Gamma_{\max}$, so $P_{\text{coherent}} \to 0$ for any finite $\tau_A > 0$.

\textbf{WKB Factor.} Conditional on coherence surviving, the tunneling amplitude is:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{WKB}} = \exp\left(-\frac{2}{\hbar_\eta}\int_{S_f} \sqrt{2m_{\text{eff}}(V_{\text{eff}} - E)} \, dx\right)
\end{equation}
The effective potential at low-dimensional strata is $V_{\text{eff}} = \alpha_\eta(S_f) V_0$ with:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_\eta(S_f) = \alpha_0 \left(\frac{d_{\max}}{d_f + \epsilon}\right)^\gamma
\end{equation}
As $d_f \to 0$: $\alpha_\eta \to \infty$, $V_{\text{eff}} \to \infty$, and the WKB integral diverges, giving $T_{\text{WKB}} \to 0$.

\textbf{Combined Suppression.} Since both factors vanish independently as $d_f \to 0$:
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{eff}} \leq \min(P_{\text{coherent}}, T_{\text{WKB}}) \to 0
\end{equation}

The suppression is doubly robust: failure of either mechanism alone cannot enable tunneling through hard vetoes.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Setup}

    \item $S_0$: A 0-dimensional ``decision point'' stratum (singular, $d=0$)
    \item $\partial S_{02}$: The 1-dimensional boundary between them
    \item Two agents, Alice ($A$) and Bob ($B$), jointly facing a decision
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Scenario}: Alice and Bob must decide whether to report a colleague's minor misconduct. The decision point ($S_0$) is the moment of filing/not-filing. The deliberation space ($S_2$) has axes: (loyalty to colleague, duty to institution).

\subsection{State Space}

The stratified Hilbert space is:
\begin{equation}
\Hil = \Hil_{S_2} \oplus \Hil_{\partial} \oplus \Hil_{S_0}
\end{equation}

Basis states in $S_2$:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\text{loyalty-high, duty-low}}, \ket{\text{loyalty-low, duty-high}}, \text{etc.}
\end{equation}

Basis states in $S_0$:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\text{report}}, \ket{\text{not-report}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Initial State}

Alice and Bob begin in $S_2$ in a superposition reflecting their uncertainty:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\Psi_0} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\ket{HL}_A\ket{HL}_B + \ket{HL}_A\ket{LH}_B + \ket{LH}_A\ket{HL}_B + \ket{LH}_A\ket{LH}_B\right)
\end{equation}
where $HL$ = (high loyalty, low duty) and $LH$ = (low loyalty, high duty).

\subsection{Transition Amplitude Calculation}

To compute the transition amplitude from $S_2$ to $S_0$, we use the boundary propagator.

The transition amplitude for Alice to move from state $\ket{HL}$ in $S_2$ to $\ket{\text{not-report}}$ in $S_0$ is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}_{HL \to \text{n-r}} = \bra{\text{n-r}}\hat{T}\ket{HL} = \int d\sigma \, \bra{\text{n-r}}e^{-i\Lag_{\text{boundary}}\sigma/\hbar_\eta}\ket{HL}
\end{equation}

For our toy model, assume the boundary Lagrangian gives:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{A}_{HL \to \text{n-r}} &= 0.9 e^{i\phi_1} \\
\mathcal{A}_{HL \to \text{report}} &= 0.3 e^{i\phi_2} \\
\mathcal{A}_{LH \to \text{n-r}} &= 0.3 e^{i\phi_3} \\
\mathcal{A}_{LH \to \text{report}} &= 0.9 e^{i\phi_4}
\end{align}

Phases $\phi_i$ are determined by the action integral along paths through moral space.

\subsection{Interference Effect}

If Alice is in superposition $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{HL} + \ket{LH})$ before transitioning, the amplitude to reach $\ket{\text{report}}$ is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}_{\to \text{report}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(0.3 e^{i\phi_2} + 0.9 e^{i\phi_4}\right)
\end{equation}

The probability depends on the phase difference $\Delta\phi = \phi_4 - \phi_2$:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{report}} = \frac{1}{2}\left(0.09 + 0.81 + 2 \cdot 0.3 \cdot 0.9 \cos(\Delta\phi)\right) = 0.45 + 0.27\cos(\Delta\phi)
\end{equation}

For $\Delta\phi = 0$ (constructive): $P_{\text{report}} = 0.72$

For $\Delta\phi = \pi$ (destructive): $P_{\text{report}} = 0.18$

\textbf{Interpretation}: The moral frameworks (loyalty-based vs. duty-based) interfere constructively or destructively depending on their phase relationship, which is determined by the boundary Lagrangian.

\subsection{Entanglement and Collective Responsibility}

If Alice and Bob become entangled through joint deliberation (exchanging transition ethons at the boundary):
\begin{equation}
\ket{\Psi_{AB}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\ket{\text{report}}_A\ket{\text{not-report}}_B + \ket{\text{not-report}}_A\ket{\text{report}}_B\right)
\end{equation}

This state has the property that measuring Alice's decision \textit{instantaneously} determines Bob's---not through communication, but through the entanglement structure.

Responsibility measurement in different bases yields correlations:
\begin{equation}
E(\theta_A, \theta_B) = -\cos(\theta_A - \theta_B)
\end{equation}

For the standard CHSH test, we use angles $a = 0$, $a' = \pi/2$, $b = \pi/4$, $b' = 3\pi/4$:
\begin{align}
S &= E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b') \\
&= E(0, \pi/4) - E(0, 3\pi/4) + E(\pi/2, \pi/4) + E(\pi/2, 3\pi/4) \\
&= -\cos(-\pi/4) - (-\cos(-3\pi/4)) + (-\cos(\pi/4)) + (-\cos(-\pi/4)) \\
&= -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = -2\sqrt{2}
\end{align}
Thus $|S| = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83 > 2$, violating the classical CHSH bound.

\textbf{Caveat}: This is the theoretical maximum. Experimental realizations would show smaller violations due to imperfect state preparation and measurement noise.

\subsection{Decoherence to Classical Limit}

At the boundary, decoherence rate increases: $\Gamma_{\partial} = \Gamma_0(d_{\max} - 1 + 1) = 2\Gamma_0$.

At $S_0$, decoherence is maximal: $\Gamma_{S_0} = \Gamma_0(d_{\max} - 0 + 1) = 3\Gamma_0 \to \infty$ as the veto limit is taken.

The density matrix evolves as:
\begin{equation}
\rho(t) = e^{-\Gamma t}\rho_{\text{coherent}} + (1 - e^{-\Gamma t})\rho_{\text{classical}}
\end{equation}

For $t \gg \Gamma^{-1}$, the state becomes effectively classical: no interference, no superposition, definite outcomes.

\textbf{Psychological grounding for phase values}: Why $\phi_{\text{HL}\to\text{report}} = \pi/6$ and $\phi_{\text{LH}\to\text{report}} = 5\pi/6$?

The phase difference $\Delta\phi = 5\pi/6 - \pi/6 = 2\pi/3$ reflects the \textit{psychological tension} between the two framings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Loyalty-based reasoning} ($HL$): The agent feels \textit{reluctant} about reporting. The path through moral space passes through ``betrayal'' territory, accumulating negative action. This generates a small positive phase ($\pi/6$).
    
    \item \textbf{Duty-based reasoning} ($LH$): The agent feels \textit{obligated} to report. The path passes through ``responsibility'' territory, accumulating positive action. But crucially, the duty framing also carries awareness of harm to the colleague, generating a large phase ($5\pi/6$) that partially opposes the loyalty path.
\end{itemize}

The phase difference of $2\pi/3$ (120) represents \textit{partial opposition}: the framings are neither fully aligned ($\Delta\phi = 0$) nor fully opposed ($\Delta\phi = \pi$). This matches the psychological reality that loyalty and duty considerations in whistleblowing scenarios are in tension but not completely contradictory---both aim at ``doing right,'' but via different moral logics.

\textbf{General principle}: Phases encode the ``moral direction'' of reasoning paths. Paths that feel psychologically aligned have similar phases; paths that feel opposed have phases differing by $\sim\pi$. This can be calibrated from order-effect experiments where question framing manipulates which path is taken first.

\subsection{Step 5: Include Decoherence}

Suppose Alice deliberates for time $t = 2$ seconds before reaching the boundary.

Decoherence rate in $S_2$: $\Gamma_{S_2} = \Gamma_0 \cdot (2 - 2 + 1)^1 = 0.5$ s$^{-1}$

Coherence survival probability:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{coherent}} = e^{-\Gamma_{S_2} \cdot t} = e^{-0.5 \cdot 2} = e^{-1} \approx 0.368
\end{equation}

Effective probability (mixture of quantum and classical):
\begin{align}
P_{\text{report}}^{\text{eff}} &= P_{\text{coherent}} \cdot P_{\text{report}}^{\text{quantum}} + (1 - P_{\text{coherent}}) \cdot P_{\text{report}}^{\text{classical}} \\
&= 0.368 \cdot 0.315 + 0.632 \cdot 0.450 \\
&= 0.116 + 0.284 = \boxed{0.400}
\end{align}
